Food Law

Texas Cottage Food Law

A plain-language starting point for home food founders. Reviewed July 8, 2026.

Texas cottage food rules let many people sell certain foods made at home without a standard food establishment permit. This is helpful for bakers, jam makers, candy makers, and other small food founders, but the details matter.

Texas SB 541 was signed on May 30, 2025 and took effect on September 1, 2025. It raised the annual gross income cap for cottage food sales to $150,000, adjusted annually for inflation, and expanded several rules around vendors, delivery, sampling, and labels.

What to check first

  • Whether your food is allowed. Meat, poultry, seafood, raw milk products, low-acid canned goods, ice products, and products with CBD or THC are excluded.
  • Whether your item needs temperature control for safety. Some refrigerated or frozen foods have extra registration, storage, delivery, and label requirements.
  • Whether your label includes the required disclosure and either an address or approved identification number.
  • Whether online sales include required label information before payment and are delivered as allowed by law.
Important: This article is a starter guide, not legal advice. Before selling food, review the current law and state guidance for your specific product.

Official sources: SB 541 enrolled bill text and SB 541 bill history.
